# Arete — Sport Module: Trail & Mountain Running

> Scientific basis: *Training for the Uphill Athlete* (House, Johnston, Jornet, 2019)
> and its predecessor *Training for the New Alpinism* (House & Johnston, 2014).
> This module sits on top of `core_training_science.md` and overrides it where they
> directly conflict. It covers trail, mountain, and ultra-distance off-road running.

---

## 1. Central philosophy

The defining demand of trail and mountain running is **sustained aerobic work over
varied gradient and long duration**, not top-end speed. The training philosophy follows
from that:

- **Aerobic capacity is the foundation and the ceiling.** Almost everything that
  determines mountain endurance performance is built by a large volume of low-intensity
  aerobic work. This base is developed over *years*, not a single training block — the
  planner should frame a single 18-week cycle as one layer on a multi-year arc, not a
  complete build from zero.
- **Patience over intensity.** The temptation to add hard intervals early is the
  classic error. Hard work is potent but small in dose; the base must come first and
  must dominate.
- **The body is trained as an integrated system** — aerobic engine, muscular endurance
  of the climbing/descending muscles, and the connective tissue that survives long
  descents — not just the cardiovascular system.

## 2. Training zones (House/Johnston model)

This framework uses a four-zone system anchored on AeT and AnT rather than %HRmax:

| Zone | Bound | Feel | Role |
|---|---|---|---|
| Z1 | Recovery, well below AeT | Very easy, fully conversational | Active recovery, warm-up |
| Z2 | Up to AeT | Easy, nasal-breathing, can talk in full sentences | **The bread and butter** — most volume lives here |
| Z3 | AeT to AnT | Moderate, "comfortably hard," talking breaks up | Used sparingly; the grey zone to limit |
| Z4 | AnT and above | Hard, can't sustain conversation | Small, targeted dose for top-end |

The bulk of base-phase running is **Z1–Z2**. Z3 is deliberately minimized (consistent
with the core polarized model). Z4 enters meaningfully only in the build/specific phase.

## 3. Determining thresholds without a lab

Two field protocols the planner can prescribe and then read back from Strava/HR data:

**Aerobic threshold (AeT) — heart-rate drift test.**
After a warm-up, run at a steady, controlled effort on flat or gentle terrain for ~60
minutes, holding HR as constant as feasible. Split the steady hour in half and compare
the **pace-to-HR decoupling** between first and second half. **Drift under ~5%** means
the effort was at or below AeT; substantially more drift means AeT was exceeded. Adjust
and retest to bracket the HR at which drift ≈ 5%. That HR is the practical Z2 ceiling.

**Anaerobic threshold (AnT) — sustained time-trial proxy.**
A ~30-minute all-out sustained effort (uphill or rolling, sport-specific) approximates
AnT; average HR over the final ~20 minutes is a workable AnT estimate. Lab lactate
testing is more precise where available, but this is sufficient to set zones.

## 4. Aerobic Deficiency Syndrome (ADS) — a key diagnostic

A signature concept from the book: if an athlete's **AeT sits more than ~10% below their
AnT** (in HR terms), they are over-reliant on anaerobic metabolism and have a
comparatively weak aerobic base — even if they feel fit. This is **Aerobic Deficiency
Syndrome**, and it's common in athletes who have trained mostly at moderate-hard
intensities.

**Planner action when ADS is detected:** prescribe an extended block of almost
exclusively Z1–Z2 work (sometimes months), with little to no high intensity, until the
AeT rises back toward AnT (gap narrows under ~10%). This is counterintuitive to the
athlete — they must go slow to eventually go fast — so the plan should explain the
rationale. Re-test AeT periodically to confirm it's climbing.

## 5. Vertical-aware training and load

Flat pace and flat-derived TSS **systematically undercount** the demand of climbing and
descending. For this sport:

- Treat **vertical gain (and loss)** as a primary training metric alongside time and
  distance. Many mountain athletes track weekly vert as their key volume number.
- Where Arete's TSS is computed from flat-equivalent pace, **apply an uphill correction**
  or prefer HR/time-in-zone as the load currency for hilly sessions, since pace
  collapses on steep ground while physiological cost stays high.
- Specificity means training on **terrain that matches the goal race's gradient
  profile.** A race with 3,000 m of climbing is rehearsed by accumulating climbing, not
  just flat mileage.

## 6. Muscular Endurance (ME) — the signature session type

The book's most distinctive contribution. **Muscular Endurance** is the ability of the
specific locomotor muscles to sustain force output over long durations — the quality
that fails on a long climb when the legs, not the lungs, give out. It is trained
separately from both aerobic capacity and max strength, and it is *highly
event-specific* for mountain athletes.

Two delivery modes:

- **Gym-based ME** — high-repetition, low-to-moderate load, short rest, targeting the
  posterior chain and legs (e.g. step-ups, split squats, calf work) to local muscular
  fatigue. Introduced after a max-strength block.
- **Hill/vertical ME** — the sport-specific version: sustained uphill efforts, often
  **weighted** (vest or pack) on steep grades, or long continuous climbs, producing deep
  local muscular fatigue at a *controlled aerobic* cardiovascular intensity. This is the
  bridge from gym strength to mountain performance.

**Placement:** ME is built in the **late base / build phase**, only after a foundation
of general and max strength exists. ME sessions are demanding and need real recovery —
treat them as key sessions, not filler. Two sessions with identical TSS can have very
different ME cost, so the planner should tag ME work explicitly rather than folding it
into generic load.

## 7. Strength progression (the full sequence)

Strength is periodized as a sequence, each phase enabling the next:

1. **Transition / anatomical adaptation** — moderate load, higher reps, full-body,
   tendon and connective-tissue preparation. 2–3×/week, base phase.
2. **Max strength** — heavy load, low reps (≈4–6), long rest, focus on force production
   and neuromuscular efficiency *without* hypertrophy (mountain athletes don't want
   added mass). Mid-base.
3. **Muscular Endurance (ME)** — convert that strength into sustained sport-specific
   output (§6). Late base / build.
4. **Maintenance** — minimal dose to retain strength through peak/taper.

Mountain-specific strength priorities: posterior chain, single-leg stability, calf/ankle
complex, and trunk/hip stability for rough-terrain control.

## 8. Downhill running and eccentric loading

Descending is where trail/ultra races are won, lost, and where bodies break.
Prolonged downhill running is **eccentric-dominant**, causing muscle damage (DOMS) that
generic uphill or flat training does **not** prepare the athlete for.

- Deliberately program **downhill running** to build eccentric tolerance — start short
  and moderate, progress gradually, since the damage-and-repair cycle is what builds the
  "repeated-bout" protective effect.
- Eccentric-biased strength work (slow lowering, controlled step-downs) supports this.
- For races with large descents, late-phase long runs should include **significant
  descending on tired legs**, not just climbing.

## 9. Periodization for trail/ultra

| Phase | Focus |
|---|---|
| Transition | Re-establish routine, anatomical-adaptation strength, easy aerobic volume |
| Base (long) | Maximize Z1–Z2 aerobic volume + vert; max-strength block; correct ADS if present |
| Build / Specific | Introduce ME (hill ME), event-specific vert, terrain specificity, descending; add limited Z3/Z4 if event demands it; back-to-back long days |
| Peak | Highest event-specificity: simulation efforts at goal terrain/duration, fueling rehearsal |
| Taper | 14–21 days for ultras; cut volume ~50–60%, retain some intensity and a little vert |

Base is the longest phase by design. For ultra distances, **time on feet** matters more
than pace, and the long run / long day is the cornerstone session.

## 10. Long runs and back-to-back days

- The **long run** builds durability, fat oxidation, fueling capacity, and mental
  resilience. For ultras it should approach event-relevant *duration* (not necessarily
  distance), built up gradually.
- **Back-to-back long days** (e.g. a long Saturday followed by a moderately long Sunday)
  simulate accumulated fatigue and time-on-feet without a single session long enough to
  cause excessive damage. A staple of ultra prep.
- Keep long runs predominantly Z1–Z2; their value is durability and aerobic volume, not
  intensity.

## 11. Fueling and gut training

Fueling is a **trainable variable**, especially for ultras where GI distress is a
leading cause of DNF:

- Rehearse race-day fueling (carbohydrate intake per hour, products, hydration,
  electrolytes) during long runs so the gut adapts to absorbing carbohydrate while
  running.
- Progressively increase carbohydrate intake rate in training toward the event target.
- Nail the strategy in the specific/peak phase, never improvise it on race day.

## 12. Equipment specificity

- **Poles** materially change the muscular demand on steep climbs (offloading legs,
  loading upper body). If the goal race uses or allows poles, train with them so the
  upper-body and pattern adaptation is in place.
- Train in race shoes/pack to adapt to their weight and feel.

## 13. Strength & conditioning summary for this sport

Even as a single-sport trail runner, S&C is integral (per core §8), with this emphasis:

- **Phase 1:** anatomical adaptation, full-body, connective-tissue prep.
- **Phase 2:** max strength (heavy, low-rep, no added mass).
- **Phase 3:** ME — gym ME then hill/weighted ME (the priority for mountain performance).
- **Throughout:** single-leg stability, calf/ankle resilience, trunk/hip control,
  eccentric tolerance for descending.
- **Taper:** maintenance only.

## 14. Quick session library (for plan generation)

- **Z2 aerobic run** — flat/rolling, nasal-breathing pace, 45–150 min. Base staple.
- **Long run** — Z1–Z2, duration-driven, event-relevant; the cornerstone.
- **Back-to-back long days** — long + moderately long on consecutive days (ultra).
- **Vertical/uphill aerobic** — sustained climbing at Z2, vert-focused.
- **Hill ME** — sustained steep uphill, optionally weighted, deep local fatigue at
  controlled aerobic HR; key session.
- **Downhill tolerance run** — progressive eccentric descending volume.
- **Z4 uphill intervals** — short, hard climbs (build/specific phase, event-dependent).
- **Race simulation** — goal terrain, vert, duration, and fueling rehearsed together.
- **Gym strength** — phase-appropriate (anatomical → max → ME → maintenance).

## 15. Override notes vs. core document

- Prefer **vert + time-in-zone** over flat-pace TSS as the load currency on hilly work.
- The **ADS diagnostic (§4)** can justify suppressing all high intensity for an extended
  period even when the core distribution table would allow some — base correction wins.
- **ME (§6)** is a first-class session type unique to this module; tag and recover it
  as a key session, not generic load.
- Ultra **taper runs long (14–21 days)** per core §7's ultra row.
