# Arete — Sport Module: Road Running

> Scientific basis: Jack Daniels, *Daniels' Running Formula* (VDOT system, pace zones);
> Pete Pfitzinger & Scott Douglas, *Advanced Marathoning* (LT-centric marathon build,
> medium-long runs); Arthur Lydiard (aerobic base periodization); Stephen Seiler

> road races from 5K to the marathon.

## 1. Central philosophy

Road running performance over 5K–marathon is governed by three physiological
determinants, and training is organized to develop each in turn:

1. **VO2max** — the size of the aerobic engine (ceiling on oxygen delivery/use).
2. **Lactate threshold (LT)** — the fraction of VO2max sustainable for prolonged efforts;
   **the single best predictor of distance-running performance**, and the central target
   for half-marathon and marathon athletes.
3. **Running economy** — the oxygen cost of running at a given pace; improved by volume,
   neuromuscular work, strength, and plyometrics, and the quality that separates athletes
   with similar VO2max.

The guiding logic (Lydiard's enduring contribution): **build a large aerobic base first**,
then layer faster, more specific work on top as the race approaches. Most of the year's
running is easy; the hard work is potent but small in dose (Seiler's polarized model).

## 2. The Daniels pace zones (VDOT system)

Daniels' framework assigns training paces from a single fitness value (**VDOT**, derived
from a recent race result). Each pace targets a specific adaptation:

| Zone | Name | Intensity | Primary adaptation | Typical use |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| **E** | Easy | ~59–74% VO2max; conversational | Aerobic base, capillarization, mitochondria, durability | Most weekly volume; recovery; long runs |
| **M** | Marathon | Goal marathon pace | Race-specific economy & fueling at MP | Marathon blocks; steady segments |
| **T** | Threshold | At/near LT; "comfortably hard," ~25–30 min sustainable | Raise LT — the key quality | Tempo runs; cruise intervals |
| **I** | Interval | ~95–100% VO2max; ~3–5 min reps | Raise VO2max | VO2max intervals (3–5 min) w/ equal-ish recovery |
| **R** | Repetition | Faster than I; short reps | Speed, running economy, neuromuscular | Short fast reps w/ full recovery |

A recent race result sets VDOT, which sets all paces; re-test periodically and re-derive
paces as fitness improves. **E pace is genuinely easy** — the most common error is
running easy days too fast, which compromises the hard days and flattens the polarized
distribution.

## 3. Intensity distribution

Polarized, per core §4. Concretely for road runners:

- The large majority of weekly mileage is **E pace** (plus the long run, also mostly E).
- **Quality** is concentrated in ~2 (occasionally 3) sessions per week: typically one
  threshold and one VO2max/speed session, with marathon-pace work added in a marathon
  build.
- Guard hard against turning easy days into moderate days. Easy volume *enables* the
  hard sessions; it is not wasted time.

## 4. Workout types in detail

- **Easy / recovery runs (E)** — aerobic development and recovery; the bulk of volume.
- **Long run** — extended E-pace run building endurance, fat oxidation, capillarization,
  and durability. Often ~20–30% of weekly volume; progressed gradually.
- **Marathon-pace (M) runs** — sustained or long segments at goal MP; rehearse pace,
  economy, and fueling. Central to marathon prep (Pfitzinger's long runs with MP
  finishes are a strong template).
- **Threshold / tempo (T)** — continuous tempo (~20–40 min) or **cruise intervals**
  (e.g. repeated ~5–15 min at T with short rest). Raises LT; arguably the highest-value
  session for half/full marathon.
- **VO2max intervals (I)** — ~3–5 min reps at ~95–100% VO2max (e.g. 5×3 min, 6×800 m,
  4×1000 m) with near-equal recovery. Develops the aerobic ceiling; most relevant for
  5K–10K and for raising the roof that LT then chases.
- **Repetition / speed (R)** — short fast reps (e.g. 200–400 m) with full recovery for
  economy and neuromuscular sharpness; low total volume.
- **Medium-long run** — a Pfitzinger staple: a midweek run between easy-day and long-run
  length that adds aerobic volume without a second true long run.
- **Strides** — short ~15–20 s accelerations after easy runs; cheap economy and turnover
  maintenance, usable in nearly every phase including taper.

## 5. Periodization

Lydiard-style sequence, adapted to the goal distance:

| Phase | Focus |
|---|---|
| **Base** | Build E-pace mileage to a sustainable peak; long run progression; strides; general strength. Mostly easy, with some T to maintain a threshold thread. |
| **Build / Strength-Hills** | Introduce/raise T volume; hill work for strength-economy; begin VO2max touches near the end. |
| **Specific / Peak** | Sharpen the quality most specific to the race: VO2max + R for 5K/10K; T + MP long runs for half/marathon. Highest race-specificity. |
| **Taper** | Reduce volume, keep intensity (core §7). |

Distance shifts the emphasis: **shorter races (5K/10K)** weight VO2max (I) and speed (R)
more heavily; **longer races (half/marathon)** weight LT (T) and marathon-pace endurance,
with the long run and MP work dominating the specific phase.

## 6. Event-specific guidance

- **5K / 10K** — VO2max-led. Quality skews to I and R; T still present; long run shorter
  but retained for aerobic support. Peak phase emphasizes race-pace intervals.
- **Half marathon** — LT-led. T work is the centerpiece; MP/HMP-specific long efforts;
  moderate VO2max for the ceiling.
- **Marathon** — LT + glycogen/economy-led. Cornerstones: progressively long runs (often
  to ~32–35 km), **long runs with MP finishes**, medium-long midweek runs, sustained T
  work, and **carbohydrate-fueling rehearsal** at MP. High aerobic mileage is the single
  biggest lever for most amateurs. Pfitzinger's structure (mileage + LT + medium-longs)
  is the reference template.

## 7. Mileage and progression

- **Volume is the dominant driver** of distance-running fitness for most amateurs; raising
  sustainable weekly mileage is usually the highest-yield change — within the athlete's
  durability limits.
- Progress conservatively (core §5: ~8–10%/week ceiling, deload every 3rd–4th week). The
  old "10% rule" is a rough guardrail, not physiology; tissue durability, not aerobic
  fitness, is the usual limiter — bone and tendon adapt slower than the heart and lungs.
- New or injury-prone runners: hold mileage lower and progress slower than the numeric
  ceilings imply; consider run/walk and higher easy-day frequency over fewer long hard
  sessions.

## 8. Running economy, cadence, and form

- **Economy improves with**: accumulated easy volume, strides, hill work, plyometrics,
  and heavy strength training (§9).
- **Cadence**: many runners are economical somewhere around ~170–185 spm, but optimal
  cadence is individual — nudge gently toward less overstriding rather than enforcing a
  universal number.
- Avoid prescriptive form overhauls mid-cycle; economy gains come mostly from volume,
  neuromuscular work, and strength, not from consciously re-engineering gait.

## 9. Strength & conditioning for road running

Integral even for single-sport road runners (core §8). The road-specific emphasis is
**economy and durability**, with strong evidence behind it:

- **Heavy resistance training** (e.g. squats, deadlifts, lunges, calf raises — heavy,
  low-rep, ~2×/week) improves running economy and performance **without** adding mass
  when volume is controlled. This is one of the best-supported non-running interventions
  for distance runners.
- **Plyometrics / reactive strength** (hops, bounds, pogos) improve economy via tendon
  stiffness and stretch-shortening efficiency; introduce gradually, they are high-impact.
- **Hill sprints** (short, steep, near-maximal) double as strength-power and economy work
  with lower injury risk than gym plyometrics for many runners.
- **Durability work** — hip/glute strength, single-leg stability, calf/foot resilience,
  and trunk control — addresses the common runner injuries (knee, shin, Achilles, hip).
- **Periodization** (core §8): general strength in base → heavier max-strength + power in
  build → maintenance through peak/taper. Heavy lifting is reduced, not eliminated, late;
  abandoning it entirely sacrifices the economy gains.

## 10. Tapering (road specifics)

- **5K/10K**: ~7–10 days, volume −~30–40%, keep sharpness with R/I touches and strides.
- **Half/marathon**: ~10–14 days, volume −~40–50%, retain a few MP/T efforts so the legs
  stay tuned to race pace.
- Maintain run frequency and some intensity; it's volume that drops. Strides throughout.

## 11. Quick session library (for plan generation)

- **Easy run (E)** — conversational, aerobic base; most volume.
- **Long run** — E pace, progressed; add MP finish for marathon prep.
- **Medium-long run** — midweek aerobic volume between easy and long.
- **Tempo / threshold (T)** — 20–40 min continuous, or cruise intervals at LT.
- **Marathon-pace (M)** — sustained MP segments; fueling rehearsal.
- **VO2max intervals (I)** — 3–5 min reps at ~95–100% VO2max; 5K/10K priority.
- **Reps / speed (R)** — short fast reps, full recovery; economy & turnover.
- **Strides** — 4–8 × ~20 s after easy runs; nearly every phase.
- **Hill sprints** — short steep near-max efforts; strength-economy.
- **Strength session** — phase-appropriate heavy lifting + plyometrics/durability.

## 12. Override notes vs. core document

- Use **VDOT-derived paces** as the concrete prescription mechanism; re-derive as the
  athlete's race results improve.
- For 5K/10K, the hard-intensity share can sit at the **upper end** of core §4's peak-phase
  range (VO2max-led racing); for the marathon, keep it lower and let **T + MP volume**
  carry the specific phase.
- Flat-road TSS maps cleanly onto pace here (unlike the trail module), so standard
  TSS/CTL/ATL/TSB guardrails apply directly without vert correction.
