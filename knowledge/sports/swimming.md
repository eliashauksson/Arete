# Arete — Sport Module: Swimming

> Scientific basis: Ernest Maglischo, *Swimming Fastest* (the canonical
> exercise-science text on competitive swimming); Sheila Taormina, *Swim Speed Secrets*
> (propulsion/technique); USA Swimming training-system literature; and the Critical Swim
> Speed (CSS) field-testing approach (Wakayoshi et al.) for threshold anchoring. This
> module sits on top of `core_training_science.md` and overrides it where they directly
> conflict. It covers pool and open-water swimming; triathlon-swim specifics also appear
> in the triathlon module.

---

## 1. Central philosophy — technique dominates

Swimming differs from running and cycling in one decisive way: **technique and economy
matter more than raw aerobic fitness**, to an unusual degree. Water is ~800× denser than
air, so drag is enormous and propulsive efficiency is everything. A fitter swimmer with
a poor stroke is routinely beaten by a less-fit swimmer with an efficient one.

The practical consequence for plan generation: **technique work is not optional warm-up
filler — it is primary training.** A swim plan that maximizes aerobic volume while
ignoring stroke mechanics will underperform. The core document's "aerobic base first"
principle still holds, but it is *co-equal* with technique development here, not ahead
of it.

## 2. Threshold anchor — Critical Swim Speed (CSS)

The practical threshold anchor is **CSS** — the swimming analogue of FTP or LT pace,
representing roughly the fastest sustainable steady pace. It's the reference for nearly
all pace-based prescription.

**CSS field test:** after a thorough warm-up, swim a maximal **400 m** and a maximal
**200 m** (well rested between). Then:

```
CSS pace (per 100 m) = (400 − 200) distance ÷ (T400 − T200) time
                     = 200 m ÷ (T400 − T200) seconds
```

This yields a threshold pace per 100 m that defines training zones. Re-test every ~4–6
weeks. (Lab lactate or a coached threshold set is more precise, but CSS is robust and
self-administrable.)

## 3. Training zones (CSS-based)

| Zone | Relative to CSS | Role |
|---|---|---|
| Recovery | well slower than CSS | Easy swimming, technique, recovery |
| Aerobic / Endurance | ~5–12 s/100 slower than CSS | Base volume; most aerobic work |
| Threshold | ~CSS ± 2 s/100 | Raise CSS directly; the key fitness driver |
| VO2max | faster than CSS | Short hard reps; aerobic ceiling |
| Sprint / Race-pace | maximal | Speed, race-pace specificity |

Most volume sits in recovery/aerobic; threshold work is the central fitness lever;
VO2max and sprint are smaller, sharper doses. The polarized logic of core §4 applies.

## 4. Key metrics

- **Pace per 100 m** — the load and prescription currency, anchored to CSS.
- **Stroke rate (SR)** — strokes per minute; and **stroke length / distance-per-stroke
  (DPS)** — distance covered per stroke. Speed = SR × DPS; efficient swimmers maximize
  DPS rather than just thrashing faster.
- **SWOLF** — (time for a length + strokes for that length); a lower score = more
  efficient. Useful for tracking economy over time.
- **Stroke count** per length — a simple efficiency proxy in training.

## 5. Session structure

A swim session is conventionally built from blocks, and plans should generate them this
way rather than as a single undifferentiated swim:

1. **Warm-up** — easy mixed swimming to prepare shoulders and feel for the water.
2. **Drill / technique set** — stroke-correction drills (see §6).
3. **Pre-set / activation** — build efforts, short fast bursts.
4. **Main set** — the day's purpose (threshold, VO2max, endurance, sprint).
5. **Cool-down** — easy swimming to flush.

Sets are prescribed on the **pace clock / send-off times** (e.g. "10 × 100 on 1:40"),
where the send-off interval controls rest and therefore intensity — a defining feature
of swim prescription.

## 6. Technique and drills

Because technique is primary (§1), drills are programmed in every session, targeting the
components of an efficient stroke:

- **Body position & balance** — high, horizontal, minimal drag (e.g. kick-on-side,
  balance drills).
- **The catch & high-elbow pull** — the propulsive heart of the stroke (e.g. sculling,
  catch-up, fist drill, single-arm).
- **Rotation** — driving the stroke from the core/hips, not just the arms.
- **Breathing** — bilateral breathing, minimal disruption to body position.
- **Kick** — propulsion and balance; ankle flexibility is a common limiter (kick sets,
  vertical kicking).

Drill choice should target the individual's specific stroke fault where known, not a
generic rotation. Technique work is most effective when **fresh**, so place it early in
the session, not buried after a fatiguing main set.

## 7. Workout / set types

- **Endurance set** — longer reps at aerobic pace (e.g. 5 × 400, 3 × 800); base volume.
- **Threshold / CSS set** — moderate reps near CSS with short rest (e.g. 10 × 100 @ CSS
  on tight send-off, or 5 × 200); the key fitness driver.
- **VO2max set** — shorter hard reps with more rest (e.g. 8 × 50 fast, 6 × 100 hard).
- **Sprint set** — maximal short efforts, full recovery; speed and neuromuscular work.
- **Pull set** — pull buoy (± paddles) to isolate upper-body propulsion and feel.
- **Kick set** — board or streamline kick; propulsion, balance, ankle mobility.
- **Drill set** — technique focus (§6).
- **Broken-distance / race-pace** — race distance in segments at goal pace with short
  rest; race specificity.

## 8. Periodization

| Phase | Focus |
|---|---|
| **Base** | Aerobic volume + heavy technique investment; raise CSS via threshold sets; build comfortable distance |
| **Build** | More threshold + introduce VO2max; maintain technique; race-pace touches |
| **Specific / Peak** | Race-pace and broken-distance work; sharpen speed; event-specific (open-water if relevant) |
| **Taper** | Swimmers taper notably (see §13) |

Frequency matters in swimming more than in other endurance sports: **feel for the
water** degrades quickly with time off, so frequent shorter swims beat infrequent long
ones for maintaining technique — important for time-limited and multisport athletes.

## 9. Open-water specifics

For open-water and triathlon swimmers, pool fitness must be supplemented with
open-water-specific skills:

- **Sighting** — lifting the eyes to navigate without a lane line; practiced into the
  stroke rhythm.
- **Drafting** — swimming on feet/hip of another reduces effort substantially; a key
  race tactic.
- **Mass-start / contact** — tolerating jostling and variable pace.
- **Wetsuit swimming** — alters buoyancy and body position; train in it if the race
  allows it.
- **Straight-line swimming** and pacing without walls/clock feedback.

## 10. Volume and the low-impact note

Swimming is **non-impact** (no eccentric/bone loading), so it carries low musculoskeletal
injury risk from volume *except* at the shoulder (§11). It can therefore serve as
recovery-supporting aerobic volume for athletes whose other sports are impact-limited —
a useful property in multisport planning (core §12).

## 11. Injury — shoulder health is the priority

The dominant swimming injury is **shoulder overuse ("swimmer's shoulder")** — rotator-cuff
and impingement issues from high stroke volume. Plan-level mitigations:

- Progress volume and **paddle use** conservatively (paddles amplify shoulder load).
- Prioritize stroke technique that reduces impingement (high-elbow catch, proper
  rotation, avoiding thumb-first entry).
- Program **shoulder-stability and posterior-chain dryland work** (§12) as injury
  prevention, not optional.
- Treat shoulder pain as a stop-and-modify signal (core §9), not something to swim
  through.

## 12. Strength & conditioning / dryland

Integral per core §8, with a swimming-specific emphasis on **shoulder integrity and
pulling power**:

- **Shoulder stability & rotator cuff** — band work, scapular control, external rotation;
  the highest injury-prevention priority.
- **Pulling strength** — lats and posterior chain (pull-ups, rows, cable/band pull
  patterns) transfer to propulsion.
- **Core / trunk** — anti-rotation and stability for an efficient, connected stroke and
  body line.
- **Ankle mobility** — limits kick propulsion; include mobility work.
- **Periodization** (core §8): general strength → strength/power → maintenance; dryland is
  year-round given its injury-prevention role.

## 13. Tapering

Swimmers have a pronounced **taper culture** and respond strongly to it. A typical swim
taper is **~10–21 days**, with substantial volume reduction (often 40–60%+) while
**retaining race-pace speed** through short, sharp efforts. The combination of large
volume reduction and maintained intensity reliably produces a performance "supercompensation"
in swimmers — lean toward a fuller taper here than in running, where the same magnitude
risks staleness.

## 14. Quick session library (for plan generation)

- **Technique/drill set** — stroke-fault-specific drills, done fresh.
- **Endurance set** — 5 × 400 / 3 × 800 at aerobic pace.
- **CSS / threshold set** — 10 × 100 @ CSS on tight send-off; the key fitness session.
- **VO2max set** — 8 × 50 / 6 × 100 fast with more rest.
- **Sprint set** — maximal 25s/50s, full recovery.
- **Pull set** — pull buoy ± paddles (conservative paddle progression).
- **Kick set** — board/streamline kick; ankle mobility.
- **Race-pace / broken** — goal-distance in segments at race pace.
- **Open-water skills** — sighting, drafting, wetsuit (if relevant).
- **Dryland** — shoulder stability + pulling strength + core.

## 15. Override notes vs. core document

- **Technique is co-equal with aerobic volume**, not subordinate to it — uniquely among
  the endurance modules. Always program technique/drill work.
- **CSS pace** is the native load currency; swim-specific TSS can be derived from CSS
  threshold pace if a single load number is needed for the PMC, but pace-per-100 is the
  prescription unit.
- **Frequency > duration** for maintaining feel for the water — favor more frequent
  shorter swims, especially for time-limited athletes.
- **Fuller taper** than running (large volume cut + retained speed) is the swim-specific
  norm.
- **Shoulder health** is the governing injury constraint; paddle and volume progression
  are gated by it.
